# Cover Letter Principles

Specific to STEM PhD students applying to technical roles at defense primes, national labs, FFRDCs, nuclear companies, and hard tech startups. Not generic advice. These principles come directly from building 70+ tailored application packages and understanding what actually differentiates a letter in this applicant pool.

---

## The Three-Paragraph Structure

Every cover letter in this toolkit uses the same structure. Not because it is the only structure that works — because it is the one that answers the three questions every hiring manager is actually asking, in the order they ask them.

**Paragraph 1 — Why you specifically for this role**

Lead with the most technically specific connection you can make. The reader's first question is: does this person actually have the background this role requires, or are they carpet-bombing with a generic letter? Answer it in the first two sentences.

Not: "I am a PhD student in materials science and I am excited about the opportunity to contribute to your team."

Instead: "My current research produces processing window predictions for CMC laminates using MD-parameterized continuum models — the same modeling approach your AFRL-funded work on SiC-SiC turbine hot sections requires for qualification support."

The reader should not be able to swap your first paragraph with another applicant's. If they can, rewrite it.

**Paragraph 2 — Evidence**

One or two concrete accomplishments with quantitative specificity. Not a list of skills. Not a list of courses. What did your work produce, and what are the numbers?

"I developed a processing model that identified a cure cycle (155-165°C, 0.8-1.2 MPa, 90 min) that increases interlaminar shear strength by 23% relative to the baseline industry cycle. The model was validated against 40 experimental specimens and is currently being used by my collaborators at [partner institution]."

If you have an active clearance and are applying to a cleared role, state it here in one sentence and move on. Do not make it the centerpiece — it is evidence, not the argument.

**Paragraph 3 — Why this company specifically**

Name something specific: a program, a technology, a published result, a research direction. This paragraph does one job — it proves you read their work and did not just find them on a job board.

"I am specifically interested in your work on joining approaches for SiC-SiC components — the mismatch in thermal expansion coefficients across joint interfaces is exactly the failure mechanism I have been modeling at the atomic scale, and I would welcome the chance to connect that analysis to your qualification testing program."

Close with a specific ask. Not "I look forward to hearing from you." Something like: "I would welcome a conversation about how my current MD/DFT work on interface failure mechanisms might support the [specific program] effort."

## Lead with Specificity, Not Enthusiasm

"I am excited to apply for this position" is the weakest possible opening sentence. It tells the reader nothing about you and signals that the same sentence opened 30 other letters this morning.

Open with a technical claim or a fact that only you could have written. The test: could a different applicant from your program send this exact letter with their name swapped in? If yes, the opener needs to be rewritten.

The most effective openers in this context are:

- A specific technical connection between your current work and the company's stated technical challenge
- A direct reference to a program, publication, or product line that shows you did the research
- A statement of a result you produced that is directly relevant to what the role requires

The opener sets the frame for everything that follows. A generic opener signals a generic applicant. A specific technical opener signals someone who read the company's work before writing the letter — and that is immediately distinguishable from the majority of applicants in this pool.

## The Translation Frame

This is the most important reframe for PhD students writing to industry and national lab audiences.

Do not describe what you did. Describe what your work produced and who cared about the output.

**Weaker:** "I ran over 1,000 molecular dynamics trajectories to study thermal transport in PEEK matrix composites."

**Stronger:** "I built an MD pipeline that produced thermal conductivity predictions across the processing temperature range for PEEK/CF systems — atomic-scale calculations translated into processing specifications that my collaborators are now testing experimentally."

The difference is not modesty or immodesty. It is the difference between describing activity and describing output. A hiring manager at a national lab or defense company is asking: what would this person produce for us? The translation frame answers that question directly. The activity description forces the reader to do the work of connecting your research to their need.

Every technical description in your letter should answer two questions implicitly:
1. What did this produce? (result, specification, prediction, model, dataset)
2. Who used it or could use it? (collaborator, industry partner, manufacturing process, design decision)

If you cannot answer both questions for a given piece of your work, do not include it. It is not yet translatable.

## Own What You Don't Have

If you are applying for a role that requires manufacturing experience you do not have, process development experience you do not have, or experimental capabilities you do not have — do not hide it. Address it directly and frame it as the purpose of the internship.

**Weak (avoidance):** Emphasizes computational work and says nothing about the manufacturing requirement.

**Weak (false claim):** Vaguely mentions "experience with materials processing" when the reality is one undergraduate lab course.

**Strong (direct ownership):** "My background is computational — I model processing-structure relationships rather than run the experiments directly. The internship is the specific opportunity to close that gap. I can bring an atomistic model of the processing physics and ground it against your manufacturing data in a way that a pure experimentalist could not."

Honesty about gaps reads as self-awareness and confidence. Hiring managers have interviewed enough PhD students to know exactly what a first-year has and has not done. An applicant who names their gap and frames it clearly is a lower risk hire than one who appears to be hiding something or does not know their own limitations.

The framing matters: the gap is not a weakness, it is the reason the internship is valuable. You are not asking them to overlook a deficiency. You are offering a specific trade that only makes sense because the gap exists.

## The Clearance Signal

If you hold an active clearance (Secret or above) and are applying to a cleared role, state it in paragraph 2, one sentence:

"I hold an active [Secret / Top Secret / TS/SCI] clearance from my work on [program / agency / prior role]."

That is the entire mention. Do not elaborate. Do not spend a paragraph explaining the nature of the work. The hiring manager understands what a clearance means. The information does its work in one sentence.

Do not bury the clearance mention at the end of the letter. It is a hard filter that eliminates most competing applicants. Put it in paragraph 2, where the evidence lives, immediately after your key accomplishment. The flow: "[Accomplishment with numbers]. I also hold an active [level] clearance. [Third piece of evidence]."

Do not make the clearance the centerpiece of the letter either. A cleared candidate with no relevant technical background is still a weak candidate. The clearance is a differentiator, not a qualification. Lead with technical substance, support it with the clearance signal, and let the combination do the work.

## Naming Specific Programs and Technologies

Every defense prime, national lab, and FFRDC has programs they are proud of, technologies they have invested in, and research directions they are actively pursuing. Name one. This is the single fastest way to separate your letter from the applicant who found the posting on LinkedIn and spent 20 minutes on the company website.

Examples of the specificity level that works:

- Lockheed Skunk Works: F-22 sustainment, SR-72 hypersonic materials, SPIDER sensor integration
- RTX Pratt & Whitney: LEAP engine ceramic matrix composite fan blades, GEAE CMC turbine shrouds (competitive intelligence)
- Northrop Grumman: B-21 Raider composite aerostructures, GBSD program materials qualification
- ORNL CNMS: in-situ TEM capabilities, neutron scattering for polymer structure
- Kairos Power: molten fluoride salt corrosion of structural materials, Hermes demonstration reactor schedule
- MITRE: DARPA ERI materials for microsystems, national security systems engineering

You do not need to know everything about these programs. You need to name one that connects to your technical work and say something substantive about why the connection matters. "I saw that your work on SiC-SiC joining for LEAP hot section components is constrained by the same thermal gradient problem I have been modeling" is not insider knowledge — it is reading the company's published literature and making the connection explicit.

## The Closing Ask

Do not close with "I look forward to hearing from you." It is passive, it is generic, and it ends the letter on the weakest possible note.

Close with a specific, active ask that demonstrates you know what you would contribute:

"I would welcome the chance to discuss how my current DFT and MD work on grain boundary segregation might support the [program name] materials qualification effort."

"I would be glad to share my processing window model in more detail — I think there is a direct connection to the parameter space your manufacturing team is currently qualifying."

"If there is a summer 2026 intern cohort forming for the [division/program] group, I would welcome a conversation about fit."

The closing ask does three things simultaneously: it restates what you would contribute (not just that you want the role), it names the specific program or effort again (reinforcing that you read their work), and it proposes a concrete next step (making it easy for the reader to act).

---

## Length

Three paragraphs. 250-350 words. One page, never more.

Hiring managers at national labs and defense primes read 200+ applications per open position during peak season. A letter that runs long signals one of two things: the applicant cannot edit, or the applicant thinks more words will compensate for a weak technical argument. Neither is a good signal.

If you find yourself writing a fourth paragraph, cut the weakest thing in paragraphs 2 or 3 and move on. The constraint is not arbitrary — it forces you to prioritize. What is the single most important accomplishment? What is the single most specific program connection? Those are the things that belong in 300 words. Everything else is noise.

## Cold Email vs. Cover Letter

Cold emails are a different format. Do not submit a cover-letter-length cold email. Under 200 words, no formal salutation, no formal close.

**Subject line:** PhD intern inquiry — [technical area] / [your institution]

**Opening:** No "Dear [name]," and no "I am writing to express my interest." Start with a sentence that explains who you are and contains a specific technical reference to their work.

"I'm a first-year materials science PhD at [institution] working on processing-structure relationships in CMC systems — I came across your AIAA paper on SiC-SiC joining and wanted to reach out."

**Middle (2-3 sentences):** What you are working on that is relevant. What you could contribute. Specific, not generic.

**Close (1 sentence):** A single low-friction ask. "Is there a summer 2026 intern program I should apply to, or would a 15-minute call be possible?"

No attachments on the first email. No formal closing ("Sincerely," "Best regards,"). Sign with your name, institution, and a link to your GitHub or Google Scholar if either is active and represents your work well.

The cold email does one job: get a response. Not a job offer, not a commitment — a response. Keep it short enough that the reader can finish it in 30 seconds and reply in 30 more.
