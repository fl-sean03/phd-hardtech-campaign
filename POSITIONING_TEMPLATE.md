*Why clearance matters: Many defense, national lab, and aerospace internships require US citizenship
or an active clearance. Some postings are open to cleared candidates only and will not consider
others regardless of technical qualifications. Clearance status is a hard qualifier — agents use
this to flag roles where you will be disqualified before your resume is reviewed.*

*If you have no clearance but are a US citizen: note that explicitly. Many programs will sponsor
clearance investigation for the right candidate. Agents will frame your citizenship as eligibility
for future clearance, which is a meaningful positive signal for defense-adjacent roles.*

## 7. Your Core Thesis

*This is the most important section. Read it carefully before writing.*

*Your core thesis is the one-sentence answer to: "Why are you uniquely positioned to solve a problem
that matters to the employers you're targeting?" It is NOT a job objective. It is not "I am seeking
a summer internship." It is a research-grounded value proposition.*

*It should be specific enough that it could only describe you — not every other PhD student in your
field.*

*Examples:*
- *"I build computational tools that predict failure in metallic alloys at scales too large for DFT
  and too small for FEA — the exact regime where aerospace and defense programs lose confidence in
  their material models."*
- *"I combine machine-learned force fields with high-throughput screening to identify battery
  cathode materials 100x faster than conventional DFT, directly addressing the bottleneck in
  solid-state battery development programs at national labs and energy companies."*

```
Core Thesis:
  [YOUR CORE THESIS — write 1-3 sentences. Be specific. Name the problem. Name the scale or
   regime. Name the application domain. This sentence will open your cover letters.]
```

*Once you've written this, read it aloud. If it sounds generic, make it more specific. Agents will
use this exact framing as the foundation of every cover letter they write for you.*
